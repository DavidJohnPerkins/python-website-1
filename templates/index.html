My Skills

Relational Database Development

Data can be a dirty word these days, but there's no getting away from the stuff. Data for data's sake is a bad thing - it will sit there, bloated, lurking, consuming energy on a server farm somewhere in Kazakhstan until the moment it can be used to screw somebody's life up while earning a criminal (either organised or, more seriously, one who runs a social media platform) a few cents. On the other hand, data can be lean, match-fit, secure and necessary and, to the trained observer, a thing of structural and design genius. Getting it right can be difficult - rather like the Times crossword - and an elegant solution to a thorny problem can still leave me smiling even after 25 years in the game. Like that crossword, SQLServer and PostGres offer up their own cryptic frustrations, but so as the practiced puzzle-solver comes to know the setter so the experienced developer understands how to approach the problem given the clues and will always be able to fill in that final square while wearing a satisfied smile. And let's not forget that a tidy worker is a better worker, and so time spent laying out the code with consistent indentation and capitalisation is never time wasted. Anything else is simple laziness and a source of frustration for those who come along later.

The City of London and What It Can Offer

Money has always been a dirty word, but there's no getting away from the stuff. As a financial journeyman, having already travelled the world of world travel by developing systems for travel agents, I ventured south and into the world of investment management systems. Bewildered by what was effectively a foreign language, I sought knowledge and learned things they could only teach me of in college (while listening to The Police on the way home from night school) and qualified three months later as FSA Registered Representative for non-derivative securities. I was thus allowed to work as a bond trader (when many of those creatures were failing that very same examination and losing their jobs), but a short conversation with a desk head at ANZ put paid to that ambition (assume Aussie twang) 'David...you simply aren't rude enough to do this job...' Nonetheless, I moved through fixed income back offices, swap trading desks (where rudeness is raised to an exquisite artform), FX support desks...you name it...and was then spat out onto Threadneedle Street after 18 years at a major Canadian bank (you do the math). I dusted myself down and entered the world of fiduciary management and pensions, and then paid homage at the London Stock Exchange where I learned the outs and very many ins of bond index construction. Curiosity then took me very briefly - not even the blink of an eye - into the tabloid crossword world of Google BigQuery (after which I felt almost the need for disinfection) and now I work within the rigour of retail finance where you are cautious and very, very thorough. This is clean money - your money and mine - and getting it wrong simply isn't an option.

The Ability to Communicate Without a Touchscreen and to Use the Apostrophe Reliably

Grammar, punctuation and fully-formed sentences are dirty words these days, and there is plenty of getting away from this stuff. In the same way that good database design and code can be both intellectually sound and aesthetically satisfying, the written word can both inform and entertain even when meaning is abstract or obscure, and the strengths of both disciplines lie in careful execution and the best possible use of available resources. Language is an exquisite innovation, available only our species, and yet it is abused relentlessly and taken recklessly for granted (please note the careful sidestepping of the split infinitive). Of course there are disciplines where precise, accurate language is the norm - the law being the prime example - but why does correct usage now seem to be the exception rather than the rule? Many might argue that strict adherence to grammatical rules doesn't make sense (and they would be right because English grammar itself is heavily contrived and largely nonsensical) and that there are plenty of exemplars of our language who have started at least one sentence with a preposition. I don't seek purity, but I do like reading prose that bears the watermark of somebody who has at least made an effort regarding clarity and the observation of the basic rules of spelling and punctuation. For myself, I take pride in my written work, both for professional and personal purposes, and I consider myself an accomplished verbal and written communicator simply because I enjoy practicing the discipline so much.
